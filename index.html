A Portrait of the American 'Melting Pot'

The United States has historically held the reputation of being a land of opportunity; as such, immigrants have long flocked to the nation in hopes of establishing new and better lives.
Despite recent political turmoil and U.S. leadership that have at times altered both the perception and process of immigrating to America, the U.S. remains "a nation of immigrants."
Data from the U.S. Decennial Census and the American Community Survey show the past and present of America's cultural landscape, as well as how that terrain is now changing.

Language Statistics for Americans Age Five and Above (2013)

While the United States has no official language at the federal level, English is its de facto national language. Besides English, many other languages are spoken, with at least 380 languages or language groups in use by members of the U.S. population.

Future Projections

One common and quick measure of the nation's cultural landscape is that of majority-minority status. The Census Bureau made headlines when it released the graphic above, which shows their prediction that white Americans will lose majority status in the year 2044, a pivotal moment vehemently feared by white nationalists and eagerly anticipated by those who see it as an end to Republican dominance.
However, some researchers believe the Census Bureau's prediction is dangerous in its portrayal of demographic change as a zero-sum game with white Americans as the losers and even inaccurate in who it counts as "white."
Regardless, there is no doubt that America needs to reckon with its increasingly diverse population on both societal and individual levels - particularly now, in a time of heightened political polarization and racial tensions.
(Source: 2014 National Projections and The New York Times)
